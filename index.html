On a Friday morning in Navalmoral de la Mata, I was born into a country in the middle of the most important transformation of the 20th century. Spain was transitioning from nearly four decades of dictatorship to democracy. The streets buzzed with hope and uncertainty in equal measure.

That same year, Spain would approve its Constitution — the document that guaranteed the freedoms my parents' generation had only dreamed of. The economy was turbulent: inflation at nearly 20%, but there was an optimism that couldn't be quantified.

My school years coincided with one of the most ambitious educational reforms in Spanish history. Under the EGB system (Educación General Básica), I navigated from preschool through eight years of mandatory education while Spain was reinventing itself.

In 1985 Spain joined the European Economic Community, setting off waves of modernization. By 1990, the LOGSE law extended compulsory education to age 16 — a seismic shift. Spain's dropout rate, hovering around 40%, was nearly double the European average.

My teenage years unfolded against the backdrop of Spain's darkest modern chapter. The country held the highest AIDS rate in Western Europe. Heroin had devastated an entire generation — nearly 2,000 deaths per year at its peak. Youth unemployment hit a staggering 42% in 1994.

Yet there was light: the 1992 Barcelona Olympics and Seville Expo put Spain on the world stage. Grunge music, early mobile phones, and Windows 95 arrived. I navigated BUP and COU (the pre-university system), repeated a year, and emerged into an uncertain world.

After repeating COU, I moved to Extremadura for university. Spain was experiencing an economic boom — GDP growth averaged 4.2% and the internet was transforming everything. From 1% penetration in 1997 to 20% by 2002, the digital revolution was rewriting the rules.

I explored university life but ultimately didn't complete my degree. What I found instead was a passion for design and visual communication that would define the next decade of my career. Sometimes the detour is the destination.

In November 2002, I joined Kopybasa as a graphic designer and printer. What followed was nine years of honing my craft — from brand identity to digital printing, managing clients and projects. I was building skills I didn't yet know would be the foundation for everything that came next.

Meanwhile, Spain rode the biggest housing bubble in its history. Prices surged 197%. When Lehman Brothers collapsed in 2008, the shockwave hit Spain particularly hard — unemployment rocketed from 8% to 27%. By the time I left Kopybasa in 2011, I was ready to bet on myself.

The Entrepreneur

"Two businesses, a pandemic, and a decade of building from scratch."

🚀 Clownfish Studio / Koralium
0 Projects (Clownfish)

In April 2013, in the depths of Spain's worst economic crisis, I co-founded Clownfish — a graphic design and digital studio. Most people thought I was crazy. Over 9 years, we delivered 100+ projects across branding, web design, and e-commerce. In 2015, I co-founded Koralium, an organic food supermarket managing 500+ SKUs.

Then COVID hit. Spain's GDP plummeted 11.3% in 2020, the worst contraction in modern history. But e-commerce surged 36%, validating the digital skills we'd been building. By September 2022, after nearly a decade, I wound down both businesses — not from failure, but ready for the next chapter.
